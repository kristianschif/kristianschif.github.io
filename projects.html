Raw Material Automation

The goal of this project was to propose a solution for composite raw material handling and storage of the Blades Product Center. The solution must ensure that all composite raw material in the freezer and on the shop-floor is conforming to company and industry specifications to minimize potential escapes; lowering costs associated with expired and nonconforming material. The team I worked with successfully benchmarked three different software solutions in the composite tracking and nesting industry. Based on user experience with the software, a proposal has been developed for the approval of upper management to implement a new kiosk system onto the shop-floor.

Mistake Proofing

During the summer of 2017, the Quality team started a project initiative to discover and develop preventative measures for recurring issues within the Blades Product Center. The goal of this project was to develop a step-by-step mistake proofing process that the entire product center would utilize. For roughly a month, the Quality team met, brainstormed, and developed a standardized procedure that all product center employees could seamlessly implement. The result of our efforts was superb; a standard work that was easy to follow and provided users with powerful tools such as 5-Whys and Fishbone Diagram. Through this Root Cause and Corrective Action (RCCA) approach, the Blades Product Center has been able to uncover and resolve many of the recurring discrepancies the product center faces.

Work-in-Progress (WIP) Inventory Reduction Effort

During my summer 2017 internship, I assisted a coworker with the task of tracking and minimizing all work-in-progress (WIP) at the Blades Product Center. At the initiation of this project back in 2015, it was discovered that the product center was housing inventory worth roughly $40 million that was either unaccounted for or stuck in production. The project focused on developing a Microsoft Access database to identify all of the WIP inventory that existed in the center so action could be taken to progress it through production. With inventory dating back to 2014, a great deal of data mining was required to identify each piece of inventory and their respective statuses to clear them for production. From the team’s efforts, the WIP Inventory has been substantially reduced by roughly $14 million.

Senior Design: United Technologies (UTC)

For my senior design project I am partnered with three electrical engineers, three mechanical engineers, and one other MEM student to solve an issue the UTC's Climate Control and Security (CCS) division is facing. The primary objective of this project is to investigate current technology and develop a means of weight detection for two different problems. Solutions to these issues will yield cost savings connected to energy waste, excessive manual labor, and overhead inefficiency.

Left to Right: William Harasyko (MEM), Kristian Schif, Mousumi Roy (Advisor)

The first problem the company has asked us to investigate is the detection of mass within a refrigerated cargo truck. There is no cargo detection system currently in place to regulate the climate control system of these vehicles. The current control system is done manually by an inspector who determines whether to power the refrigerant system on or off. As it stands, the vehicles are similar to the a standard 48ft by 9ft by 8ft tractor trailer truck with built in climate control systems. Therefore, roughly 4704 ft3 must be controlled and kept within certain specifications, leading to high energy consumption. Currently, there is no Boolean weight detection system built-in to the vehicle. Hence, the system will often run at unnecessary times (e.g. empty during inspection or in transit to loading station).

The second problem we were tasked was to investigate a possible solution to detect fluid levels within CCS's fire suppression systems. The suppression tanks used in these systems need to be inspected biannually to ensure each tank has sufficient fluid levels. If the fluid level within a tank is reduced by 2.5% from the allowable value, it must be replenished to comply with specification. This current inspection process is both manual and extremely time consuming; weighing each of the tanks individually by detaching them from their respective hoses, lifting it onto a scale, and comparing the value to the one previously recorded. CCS is seeking to automate this process to improve the accuracy and mitigate the costs of this inspection.

In December of 2017, based on preliminary research and experimentation, the team officially decided to pursue the fire suppression tank project. This project seeks to develop a working prototype that can accurately detect the mass contained within the enclosed fire suppression tanks. The phenomena of resonant frequencies has been investigated and tested to determine the current level within the tank. From experimentation, it has been discovered that the natural frequency of the entire system changes as the amount of fluid content fluctuates. This relationship was experimented on to derive a model that can output accurate information as to the fluid level within the tank.

Process Map for Suppression Tank Device

The final deliverable of the project is to develop a mass sensing system prototype that can be implemented small scale. This system consists of a motor-controlled impact device that would induce vibrations of the tanks, a sonar sensor/audio-recorder that would sense the induced vibrations, a processing unit to analyze the frequency spectrum of the data, and a Bluetooth module that transmits the data to an Android mobile application. With such a system, CCS will be able to save both time and money on the current inspection process while having the capability to autonomously track the contents of its suppression tanks.

Honors Thesis: Pet Teq Company

The purpose of my honors thesis project is to investigate the development, manufacture, and introduction of a new product to the commercial market. The idea for this product originally came about from a personal experience with my pets. I have two dogs who let us know they need to go outside by hitting a bell. At certain locations in the house, the bell cannot be heard and the dogs will end up going to the bathroom inside. Looking for a solution to this problem, my brother and began to brainstorm. Our first idea was to develop a wireless mat that would send a notification to the owner’s smartphone when it was pressed; it would be called the “Doggie Talkie”! Process Diagram for Original Product

To bring the idea to life, I used rapid prototyping to test out different features and capabilities for the product. The product needed to house a Raspberry Pi microcontroller, contain contact points, and be touch sensitive. Below are the different prototypes that were made throughout the process:

Each prototype saw different design improvements. By the third prototype, we had a functioning proof-of-concept we could work with. We now needed to configure the microcontroller to the prototype so that it would send out an Apple Push Notification (APN) to the internet when it was activated. At this point, a friend of mine, Trevor Phillips, was brought onto the team; an expert in iOS development and Xcode. Please feel free to visit my teammate's personal website trevphil.com to learn more about the different experiences and projects he has been involved with.

After about a week of coding, we had an iPhone app running that allowed the user to configure the Raspberry Pi to the internet over a Bluetooth connection. Once connected properly to the internet, the device would output a notification to the user’s phone.

iPhone Application Logo

Video of Prototype Sending APN to iPhone Application

At this stage, our team was aware of the product’s limited marketability and its limitations on solving the original problem. Even if the dog could notify the owner that it needed to go outside, he or she would still need to go to the door to let the pet in and out. After some more brainstorming, our team came up with the idea of developing a “Smart Dog Door” using similar technology. The “Smart Dog Door” would be a wireless pet door that allowed the pet to go inside and outside at the owner’s discretion via a smartphone app. When the dog either approaches or sits by the front door, the owner is notified and can allow the dog to go either in or out with the click of a button that opens the door.

The team is still working to develop a prototype capable of this application, however the prototype is likely to include RFID technology for the dog’s collar, an actuator pin to prevent unwanted exit or entry, a stepper motor to lift the door, a high frequency beeper that emits a frequency that only pets can hear, and a Raspberry Pi microcontroller to control the entire application. The team has even decided to enter the idea into an entrepreneurial competition at the university in the hopes to get funding for the project. More to come on this project soon!

Autonomous Transport Vehicle

During my summer 2016 internship with Sikorsky Aircraft Corporation, I participated in a company-wide engineering competition. Ten teams consisting of five engineers from different disciplines competed in the competition.

The goal of the competition was to design an autonomous vehicle that could transport one gallon of water from Point A to Point B. The project seemed relatively easy at first until they introduced several different obstacles and design specifications:

  • The path that needed to be followed was made of a white LED strip. The LED strip eventually broke off into two pathways, one green and the other red. The Car needed to follow the Green LED strip.
  • To adhere to the competition’s design specifications, the vehicle had to be very narrow and top-heavy.
  • The vehicle could only use the two low torque DC motors that were provided.

After a week of brainstorming, we put our ideas to work. We began by creating the vehicle's framework. The DC and Servo motors were then rigged to the vehicle. Following this, we began developing a rough circuit for the vehicle using Arduino components provided by the company. Shortly after, we hit our first milestone. The vehicle could travel in a straight line!

Vehicle Framwork and Infrastructure

Early State Circuit and Configuration

Although it seems like we were very far in the project, there was a great deal of work left to do. To track the LED strip, we designed an overhang at the front of the vehicle. The overhang was rigged with four photosensors; one on the far left, left of center, right of center and far right. The job of the photosensors was to act like a sweeper arm for the LED strip and realize when the vehicle was falling off course. When falling off course, commands would be sent to the servo motor to steer the vehicle in the right direction. After countless hours of trial and error, we had our vehicle working and ready to compete at the competition.

Finished Product on Race Day

On race day, we came across several issues we had not considered when first designing our vehicle. Regardless of these challenges, our vehicle performed exceptionally well and placed fourth overall.

This project has certainly been one of the most diverse and intricate ones I have ever been a part of. It is an excellent example of how a team can unite and utilize everyone's skillset to achieve a common goal.

Texas Hold'em Simulation & Probabilities

The main purpose of this project was to investigate the practicality and importance of simulations for real-world applications. Simulations give insight into the performance and behavior of a system without the need to make them firsthand. In many situations, it is neither practical nor feasible to model a system in real life. In this scenario, performing 100,000 iterations of Texas Hold’em rounds would require an immense amount of time and effort. As a result, a simulation was developed instead to imitate Texas Hold’em hands in a practical manner.

This project was broken down into three different sections: a simulation, theoretical calculations, and a real-world experiment. Each of the results from these sections were then put through numerous hypothesis tests to validate their accuracy.

To begin, the simulation for this project was developed using Microsoft Excel’s Visual Basic (VBA) software. The simulation is programmed to run a number of iterations specified by the user. During these iterations, the program compiles the number of occurrences of each poker hand and outputs the corresponding probabilities of that hand occurring. The following are pictures of the software interface and code:

Main User Interface

Example Code

To determine the probabilities of each hand occurring, the simulation was set to run 100,000 iterations. The associated probabilities for each hand occurring from these 100,000 iterations can be found below:

Results from 100,000 Iterations of Simulation

Following this, the theoretical probabilities of each hand occurring was then calculated using combination equations. Finally, 100 trials were carried out in real-life to further validate the results. The probabilities of each hand from the simulation, theoretical calculations, and real-world can be found in the following image:

Probabilities from Simulation, Theoretical Calculation, and Real-World Experiment

Hypothesis tests were then performed to verify the results. Surprisingly, the only probabilities to fail the hypothesis test were that of Four-of-a-Kind from the real-world trials. This was due to the fact that a Four-of-a-Kind occurred during the 100 trials was an outlier. Based on theoretical calculation, a Four-of-a-Kind is supposed to occur roughly once in 589 trials.

This project was quite versatile; requiring an intense amount of VBA coding, mathematical calculation, and statistical hypothesis testing. The project allowed me to apply and demonstrate a wide range of knowledge and skills I have gained throughout my undergraduate career.

Homemade Wireless Bluetooth Speaker

If it hasn’t been made apparent, one of my favorite hobbies is creating and building. To put my skills to the test yet again, I set out to disassemble and reconfigure a Brookstone speaker system that had been sitting in my attic for years. This speaker system was cumbersome and had an unappealing design. To bring this device back to life, I needed to design a new housing unit for all of the speaker’s components with a more aesthetic appeal. On top of this, I wanted to make the unit Bluetooth compatible so that it can be used wirelessly.

I first drafted a few concept designs for this speaker on paper until I was satisfied. The next step was to create a 3D model of the design using Computer Aided Design (CAD) SOLIDWORKS. When developing the model, I needed to consider the components of the speaker, their proximity to one another, and other factors that would allow the speaker to work properly in the smallest enclosure possible. In order to make this work, I was required to make rough models of all the speaker components so that an assembly file could be made to verify that the design was feasible.

With the design complete, I was now tasked with the challenge of selecting the proper material to get the job done. I decided to use Maple wood as the primary enclosure material given that it is a very hard wood that provides a very clean finish. It was time to bring my design to life. Surprisingly, only a chop saw, routing table, drill press, and belt sander was required to get the whole project done. To give the speaker a water resistant and shiny finish, several coats of polyurethane were applied. Once the wood had dried, the pieces were drilled together and the enclosure was complete.

Front of Speaker

Back of Speaker

I then configured the internal components of the speaker. Several minor solders were made to fix broken connections and allow for the addition of a blue LED light to improve the speaker’s aesthetic appeal. An additional 5V Bluetooth to AUX converter was added to the circuit to allow for wireless use. After all was said and done, the speaker would draw approximately 30 watts of electricity when used at full power. Once a few preliminary tests were performed, the speaker was now ready for an official sound test.

Internal Components of Speaker

At first, the sound quality of the speaker was lower than I had hoped. The sound was flat, lacking bass as if the sound was emerging from a hallow shell. I then discovered online that adding polyfill stuffing to the inside of the speaker improves the sound, similar to the way adding furniture to a room makes conversation sound “full”. The results were astonishing! The speaker sounded incredible and could reach a very high volume. This has been one of my favorite projects I have ever done, especially given the amount of usage it gets in my everyday life.

Homemade Two-Stroke Bicycle

I decided to experiment with a project unlike anything I had ever undertaken. The idea arose one day when I entered my garage, only to find a broken bicycle and broken weed wacker. Before my father could throw them away, I decided to turn one man's trash into another man's treasure. I perceived this as an opportunity to learn more about the mechanical nature of these items and set out to create a motorized bicycle.

My first objective was to repair the bicycle back into a proper working condition. Since the bike frame was still intact, all I had to do was diagnose, repair and replace the broken parts to bring the bike back to life. After replacing the rear wheel rim, bike chain, and rear derailleur, the bicycle was working like new. To ensure its reliability, I took the bike for a few long distance trips across town.

Now that the bicycle was working, it was time for me to fix the broken STIHL FS80R 25cc Weed Wacker. At first, this was an extremely daunting task to set out and accomplish as I had never worked hands-on with engines in the past. I performed a great deal of research beforehand to understand the complexities of such machines. Before disassembling the engine, I performed a couple of tests to diagnose the different issues the motor had. The engine failed a compression test, yielding 50 psi upon startup (below the minimum allowable of 60 psi). Additionally, the engine failed an electricity test, therefore the ignition coil would need to be replaced. Upon disassembling the engine, it was further discovered that the piston assembly and cylinder were harshly damaged from debris and would need to be replaced. Once these issues were resolved, the engine performed like new.

Repaired and Modified STIHL FS80R Engine

The next step was to develop a working design for the motorized bicycle. The plan was to transmit the torque of the motor to the rear wheel. To begin, a motor mount needed to be created at the rear of the bike. With the use of an arc welder and square metal tubing, a sturdy motor mount was rigged to bike. Now, with the clutch and flywheel adjacent to the rear wheel, the final step is to transmit the torque from the engine to the rear wheel.

Motor Mounted to Rear Wheel of Bicycle

Unfortunately, the project was cut short due to my return to school. The project will likely be complete during the winter intersession. In the meantime, several different solutions are being researched to transmit torque to the rear wheel.

Reverse Engineering of RC Racecar

During my first semester freshman year, I was assigned a project in the Introduction to Engineering course I was enrolled in. The goal of the project was to have a team of four disassemble and reassemble a complex object. When brainstorming potential objects to reverse engineer, it dawned upon me that I owned a TACON RC Racecar that I used to race with my father that was not working properly. This project was the perfect opportunity to get to the root cause of this problem while learning a great deal about how cars work at a macro level.

TACON RC Racecar Before Disassembly

As a team, we documented each disassembly step with pictures to ensure a successful reassembly. Along the way, we discovered the many different mechanisms that were involved in such a car. Eventually, we disassembled the rear axle gearbox only to discover the root cause of the vehicle's failure. The gear box had an excessive amount of contaminants (mostly dirt) that prevented the transfer of motion from the DC motor to the rear wheels. After a quick clean and reassembly, the vehicle was working like new.

Our team's deliverable for the project was a PowerPoint over 100 slides in total, providing insight on all our findings. The professor enjoyed our project so much that he selected us to present the project to the entire lecture of roughly 500 students. This was a very interesting way to start off my college career.
